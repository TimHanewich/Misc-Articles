# The Understanding Gap
*So much velocity, so little understanding.*

For the professional of the modern era, a gap is developing between what they truly understand and what they are authoring, or "shipping."

In the past, this gap was much smaller because employees had to produce everything themselves. Producing meaningful output required extensive understanding of the topic or material; behind every finished product were countless hours spent learning the underlying system before anything could be delivered.

Now, with the help of AI, and more specifically *agents*, that level of understanding is no longer always required. AI is quickly evolving beyond knowledge-based work into *problem-solving* and *reasoning*, and that shift is happening extremely fast, with potentially serious consequences for how work will change and evolve for all of us.

## How did we get here?
For the first time ever, technology has reached a point where it is no longer *only* assisting humans in doing their jobs. Through [Chain of Thought](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11903) reasoning and training for long-duration, far-horizon tasks, modern language models (like Anthropic's **Claude Opus 4.7** or **OpenAI's GPT-5.5**) can actually *problem-solve* and reason through challenges, troubleshooting along the way to achieve the goal given to them by the user.

To put it simply: for the first time in history, the output of machines is **intelligence**. Not just information, knowledge, or help finding what you need online, but genuine intelligence applied toward reasoning through a task and accomplishing it.

We are still in the very early days of fully realizing this new capability. Several industries and sectors have already begun incorporating it:
- **AI Coding Agents** like [Anthropic's Claude Code](), [OpenAI's Codex](), and [Microsoft's GitHub Copilot]() use long-duration reasoning and goal-seeking behavior to work through tasks given by the user. They use built-in capabilities to take action toward those goals, whether through internal tools or even external systems, and they can troubleshoot and develop novel workarounds when they encounter obstacles. Many times, I have seen newcomers to these tools thoroughly impressed, or even *shocked*, by how resilient these agents are in applying themselves and achieving almost any goal you give them, no matter how "out there."
- **Deep Research** capabilities like [OpenAI's Deep Research](), [Microsoft's Researcher Agent](), and [Gemini's Deep Research]() allow AI to move beyond *answering* questions and into *investigating* them. When you delegate a research task, the agent can plan an investigation, open 50+ webpages, read through them, and synthesize a report, all within minutes. That is work a human would normally take days to perform.
- **Cybersecurity**: Modern AI security agents like [Microsoft Security Copilot](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/copilot/security/microsoft-security-copilot) act like security analysts, proactively hunting for vulnerabilities and risks by identifying patterns that expose weaknesses, then self-remediating by autonomously isolating machines, revoking credentials, and initiating forensic audits.

In all of these examples, the AI is not just *helping* a human do work, it is actually doing work *for* the human, autonomously. In years past, this would have seemed impossible because of the highly unstructured and unpredictable nature of the work. But with modern language models provided in an "agentic" package, humans can now truly delegate these tasks, including much of the "thinking" involved, to AI.

## Velocity Pressure
With so much now delegable to AI, the maximum output of a human professional seems to be multiple times greater than it was in the past. That is an amazing thing.

I'm sure you have already seen it: endless examples of "look what I made with Claude," or "check out the amazing app Codex built for me," or "I put this PowerPoint deck together using Cowork." The *velocity* of content creation is through the roof right now. Content platforms are seeing levels of output unlike anything before, with [GitHub seeing 30x more activity](https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/github-30x-repos-infrastructure-signals-proactive-agents) as one striking example.

With so much now possible with so little effort, there is mounting pressure on those *not* using these tools to keep up with those who *are*. Particularly in the current economic environment, with mass layoffs and increasing scrutiny around output, many of us feel that our productivity must scale with these tools rather than remain stagnant.

That mounting pressure to *produce* is the root cause of what I am calling "**The Understanding Gap**."

## The Understanding Gap
When you combine **increased pressure to produce** with a **decreased barrier to entry that is approaching zero**, the result is a *decreased level of understanding* of what was produced and how it was produced. In other words, a divide is forming between *what* is being produced and our *understanding of what was produced*.

Before AI, if you wanted to build a web app for your users, you had to learn programming fundamentals, front-end development, the React framework, cloud hosting, and the overall process required to achieve that goal. After AI, you can simply prompt an agent and let it figure out the rest.

Before AI, if you wanted to deliver a presentation on a newly announced product to a customer, you would have spent hours combing through announcements, testing the capabilities, brainstorming ways to communicate the key message, building visuals into a deck, and refining your talking points, *understanding* the capability, how it works, and why it matters. With AI, you can simply prompt an agent to draft a deck with speaker notes and then read the script word-for-word.

In both examples above, in the "After AI" scenario, the required level of *understanding* is near zero. The "figuring out," "reasoning," "brainstorming," and much of the thought itself can be delegated to the agent, which then hands you only the "CliffsNotes" version you need to make the final delivery, often creating the *illusion* of understanding to the people you present to. What you produced through your agent becomes a black box that you barely understand; but wow, does it look good.

At first, this may seem like a conscious choice, that staying sharp in a domain is still possible if you care enough to do it. But market dynamics suggest otherwise: what value does *understanding* really have in a world where your peers are outsourcing that understanding and producing 5x more output as a result? For every hour you spend trying to understand an underlying process, your competitors are delegating that work to an agent and producing more because of it.

## Risks of The Understanding Gap
I believe there are risks here both for humanity as a whole and for the individual. I'll focus on the individual risks here.

**The short-term risk: "getting lost deep in the forest."** Imagine you want to travel deep into a forest to experience something amazing at the center. You are unfamiliar with forest navigation, so you hire a guide to take you there. The guide handles the difficult parts for you: cutting branches, finding paths, navigating with a compass and map. But once you arrive at the center, the guide trips, hits their head, and becomes unconscious, completely incapacitated. How do you get back out? Without any understanding of forest navigation, you are stuck.

Because AI creates the illusion that you can go deep into a domain you barely know, it encourages us to jump headfirst into topics, projects, and disciplines while using AI as a heavy crutch. In the same way you can become trapped in a forest if your guide becomes incapacitated, you can end up in serious trouble if your AI begins to struggle deep into a project's lifecycle, leaving you with no way out. It is wise to have at least some familiarity with the domain you are working in.

**The long-term risk: "puppet to the machine."** If we rely too heavily on AI to do *everything* for us, not just to *produce*, but also to *think*, *reason*, and make decisions, at what point do we become little more than human puppets to artificial intelligence? I will be the first to admit that AI is *very* intelligent and can create enormous value when we defer to it in the right ways. But deferring *too* much, too blindly, will create problems.

The more we defer without questioning, the more our critical thinking skills risk erosion. And if we become nothing more than puppets to the machine, what unique value do we provide that cannot be easily reproduced by the model itself, or by another human puppet using the same model? The value of what you provide can quickly fall toward zero, degraded by the commoditized nature of what you are offering.

## Staying Relevant
Today, there are so many hard working professionals searching for ways to remain relevant and employable in this new era. I do not have all the answers, but I will share a few early thoughts while we are still only on the doorstep of what is coming:

**Tip #1: Find the right Ownership/Delegation Balance**: When using AI agent tools, we face a difficult choice: how much effort should we spend understanding *what* the AI agent is doing and *how* it is doing it? At first glance, this may seem like a fool's errand. Why concern yourself with the underlying mechanisms if the AI will always be there to solve the problem? That is a fallacy. Project Managers (PMs), for example, are responsible for directing the work of developers toward the right problems. The *best* PMs are those who work to understand the developers' work well enough to choose the best path forward for the team as a whole. The same principle applies to users of AI agents. Having some level of understanding is helpful, but knowing exactly **what level of understanding** is worth investing in allows you to strike the right balance.

**Tip #2: Choosing what to understand**: No leader will tell you they can do a better job than every person working under them. Their role is to find the right people for each job and remove obstacles so those people can do their best work. Naturally, some of their *understanding* of each domain is being *outsourced* to others, allowing the leader to spread their focus elsewhere. That is the role of delegation, and it is a wonderful thing. However, there are also moments when leaders must become deeply informed about a particular topic or domain, and that understanding later becomes critical to making the *right decisions* about where to direct their team's energy. The question of **what** to invest your time learning and understanding will be critical in the future, especially given how much we can now delegate to AI and get away with delaying.

**Tip #3: Quickly Understanding**: As more work becomes delegable to AI agents, pressure will likely increase on the individual worker to avoid becoming an isolated "specialist" in only one domain, task, or business process. Instead, many of us will be expected to do more with these tools, not only producing more, but producing across a broader range of connected disciplines. Being a *quick, adaptable learner* is going to become a highly valuable skill in the modern era. Again, this does not necessarily mean mastering every domain at a deep level, but understanding enough to be "dangerous", enough to direct your agent exactly as needed to achieve the best outcome.

## Close
This is not entirely new. CliffsNotes allowed students to fake understanding of a book. Phrasebooks allowed travelers to fake understanding of a language while abroad. Auto-correct allows writers to fake proper spelling and grammar.

Now, with AI, it is possible to fake *intelligence*.

The temptation of borrowed capability will be powerful. Many people will postpone developing understanding in a domain, choosing instead to outsource thought and comprehension to the agent. That can quickly become a slippery slope.

Stay sharp, but not in the "old way." Stay sharp in the "new way": find the symbiosis with agents, and the balance that allows *both* you and your agents to thrive over the long term. With these agents, you're still the captain of the ship; the captain had better preserve their judgment and wit.
